<Section icon={Sparkles} title="Practice Plan for Cleaner Perfect Runs">

If you want more than basic clears, you need a repeatable practice routine. The game’s timing becomes much easier once you stop rushing through failure and start using each miss as a clue. A good practice loop is short, deliberate, and focused on one cue at a time.

<Steps>
  <Step num="1" title="Play Once Without Pressure">
    Run the mini-game normally and ignore the score. Your goal is to hear where the beat actually lives.
  </Step>
  <Step num="2" title="Find the Strongest Cue">
    Identify the clearest sound or motion that always lines up with the hit window. Use that as your anchor.
  </Step>
  <Step num="3" title="Repeat Until It Feels Automatic">
    Replay the same section until your input happens before you think about it. That is when timing starts to lock in.
  </Step>
  <Step num="4" title="Add Distractions Back In">
    Once the core beat feels stable, pay attention to the camera tricks and background noise without losing tempo.
  </Step>
</Steps>

| Common Mistake | Better Fix | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Watching the animation too closely | Listen for the beat first | Cleaner timing |
| Restarting after every miss | Finish the full run and learn the pattern | Better stage familiarity |
| Ignoring tutorial layers | Use the warm-up patterns | Faster adaptation |
| Trying to perfect everything at once | Practice one mini-game segment | Steadier improvement |

The most reliable improvement comes from repetition with a purpose. Short sessions work especially well because they keep your ears fresh and reduce the urge to force a perfect run before you understand the pattern.
